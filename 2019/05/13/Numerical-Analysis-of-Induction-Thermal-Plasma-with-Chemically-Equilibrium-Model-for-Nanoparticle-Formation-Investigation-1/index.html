Numerical Analysis of Induction Thermal Plasma with Chemically Equilibrium Model for Nanoparticle Formation Investigation

Posted on 2019-05-13

ABSTRACT

A Chemically Equilibrium model of induction thermal plasma has performed to investigate the status of plasma fluid in the torch area. Computations were carried out for argon plasma under atmospheric pressure. The thermofluid and concentration fields were obtained by solving two-dimensional modeling, including continuity, momentum, pressure and energy equations. The electromagnetic fields were calculated based on the electromagnetic vector potential representation. These differential equations were solved by the SIMPLER method. In this modeling, the higher-order approximation of the Chapman-Enskog method was used for the estimation of transport properties. The numerical analysis results show that the maximum temperature in the torch area is around 10 000 K. Under such remarkable high temperature, a small part of argon atoms was ionized to argon ion at the central torch area. The present modeling would give the guidance for the further simulation of the chamber area and estimation of nanoparticle processing using induction thermal plasma.

Introduction

Induction thermal plasmas have been widely utilized for several decades for material and environmental processing, such as nanoparticle synthesis, reactive plasma spraying, surface treatment and decomposition of harmful substances. Induction thermal plasmas offer unique advantages including high enthalpy to enhance reaction kinetics, high chemical reactivity, oxidation and reduction atmospheres in accordance with required chemical reactions, and rapid quenching rate. These advantages advance the applications of induction thermal plasmas.

Induction thermal plasmas are considered to be usually used for the synthesis of nanoparticles. However, the actual synthesis process is complicated which depends on some experimental conditions and equipment size. Therefore, the numerical analysis is essential for the measurement of induction thermal plasma and giving guidance for the nanoparticle synthesis.

In the present study, a chemically equilibrium modeling of argon induction thermal plasma was developed. The thermofluid and concentration fields of torch area were obtained by solving 4 governing equations including continuity, momentum, pressure and energy equations. The electromagnetic fields were calculated based on the electromagnetic vector potential representation.

Numerical Models

The calculations used to derive the governing equations are based on the following assumptions: (i) steady-state laminar flow, (ii) axial symmetry, (iii) optically thin, (iv) negligible viscous dissipation in energy equation, (v) negligible displacement current in comparison with the conductive current, (vi) negligible flow-induced electric field, and (vii) heavy particles and electrons at identical temperature.

The fields of flow, temperature and concentration present in the induction thermal plasma flow were calculated by solving the two-dimensional continuity, momentum and energy equations.

Continuity is represented by
$$\nabla(\rho\cdot\overrightarrow{u})=0$$
where ρ is the density and u is the velocity.
Momentum is represented by
$$
\rho\overrightarrow{u}\cdot\nabla\overrightarrow{u}=-\nabla p + \nabla\tau+\overrightarrow{J}\times\overrightarrow{B}
$$
Where p is the pressure and τ is the viscous stress tensor. The last term on the right-hand side expresses the Lorentz force.
Energy is represented by
$$
\rho\overrightarrow{u}\cdot\nabla\overrightarrow{h}=-\nabla\cdot(\frac{\lambda}{C_p}\nabla h) + \nabla\tau+\overrightarrow{J}\times\overrightarrow{B}
$$
Where h is the enthalpy, λ is the thermal conductivity, Cp is the specific heat at constant pressure, and qr is the radiation loss per unit volume. The last term expresses the Joule heating.

The electromagnetic field in the present study was analyzed on the basis of the two-dimensional modeling approach, with the electric field intensity as the fundamental electromagnetic field variable. Electric field intensity is represented by
$$
\frac{\partial^2E_1}{\partial x^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\frac{\partial E_1}{\partial r})-\frac{E_1}{r^2}-\xi\sigma\omega E_2=0
$$

$$
\frac{\partial^2E_2}{\partial x^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\frac{\partial E_2}{\partial r})-\frac{E_2}{r^2}-\xi\sigma\omega E_1=0
$$

Where ξ is the magnetic permeability, ω is the angular velocity and σ is the electrical conductivity.

The governing equations were solved using Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure Linked Equation Revised (SIMPLER) algorithm[1]. The governing equations and the electric field intensity equation associated with the boundary conditions were discretized into finite differences, using the control-volume technique. Ununiform grid points 42 × 20 were used for radial and axial directions, respectively. Thermodynamic and transport properties were calculated from the temperature and compositions computed at each position in each iteration step. Results and discussion

Fig.3 and Fig.4 show the temperature field and the argon atom distribution existing in the plasma torch, respectively. The maximum temperature is around 10 000 K, meanwhile the ratio of argon atom decreases to 0.98. Fig.5 shows the electric field intensity. It presents the skin effect that the maximum of electric field intensity is located near the wall.

Conclusion

A chemically equilibrium modeling for argon induction thermal plasma under atmospheric pressure was developed. The temperature, fluid flow, concentration and electrical field in the torch area were obtained which are consistent with experiments and former numerical analysis researches.

Future work

The calculation domain would be enlarged to the chamber area by resetting the grids and boundary conditions. Furthermore, the nanoparticle processing associated with Particle In Cell model is considered to add into the present modeling.
